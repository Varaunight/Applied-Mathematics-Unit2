\subquestion

Using De Morgan's Law (\rdef{mod1:law:DeMorgan}), we can see that,
\begin{equation}	
	\boldsymbol{\sim((p \land q) ~\lor \sim p)  \equiv ~\sim(p \land q) \land p} \,. \label{2012BoolEqn}
\end{equation}

From De Morgan's Law (\rdef{mod1:law:DeMorgan}), we also know that,
\begin{equation}
	\boldsymbol{\sim(p \land q) \equiv (\sim p ~\lor \sim q)} \,.
\end{equation}

Therefore, \req{2012BoolEqn} becomes,
\begin{equation}
	\boldsymbol{\sim((p \land q) ~\lor \sim p)  \equiv (\sim p ~\lor \sim q) \land p} \,.
\end{equation}

Using Distributive (\rdef{mod1:law:Distributive}), Complement (\rdef{mod1:law:Complement}) and Identity (\rdef{mod1:law:Identity}) laws,
\begin{align}
	\boldsymbol{(\sim p ~\lor \sim q) \land p} & \boldsymbol{\equiv (p ~\land \sim p) \lor (p \land \sim q)} \nn \\
	                              & \boldsymbol{\equiv (F) \lor (p ~\land \sim q)} \nn \\
	                              & \boldsymbol{\equiv (p ~\land \sim q) \nn} \\
								  & \boldsymbol{\equiv (\sim q \land p)} \,.
\end{align}

Thus,
\begin{equation}
	\boldsymbol{\sim((p \land q) ~\lor \sim p) \equiv (\sim q \land p)} \,.
\end{equation}
